What is Transportation Problem?

The transportation problem (TP) (or transportation algorithm) is designed for a set of problems in which: (1) a product is transported at the lowest possible cost from several sources to several destinations; and (2) each source can supply a fixed number of units of the product, and each destination has a fixed demand for the product. Although the general transportation model may be used for a wide range of issues, the most well-known application is for the movement of products, from which the problem gets its name.

There are 2 types of transportation problems:

  1. Balanced transportation problem: The problem is considered to be a balanced transportation problem when both supplies and demands are equal.
  2. Unbalanced transportation problem: Unbalanced transportation is defined as a situation in which supply and demand are not equal. A fake row or a dummy column is added to this sort of issue, depending on the necessity, to make it a balanced problem. The problem may then be addressed in the same way as the balanced problem.

The goal is to create an integrated transportation system that satisfies all criteria at the lowest possible overall shipping cost. The Supply and Demand of the items must be equal, which is an essential feature in the Transportation algorithm. To achieve the lowest possible overall shipping cost, an Initial Basic Feasible Solution (IBFS) must be determined and tested for optimality - whether the result has the best outcome.

Some Different Methods to Find IBFS:

  1. North-West Corner Rule (NWCR)
  2. Least Cost Method (LCM)
  3. Vogel's Approximation Method (VAM)

Steps North-West Corner Rule:

  1. Start on the upper left corner cell of the transportation matrix and allocate minimum (A1, B1)
    1. a.Subtract this value from supply and demand of respect row and column
    2. “If supply” is zero, then cross that row and move down to the next cell
    3. If “demand” is zero, then cross that column and move right to the next cell
    4. If “supply” and “demand” both are zero, then cross both row and column and move diagonally to the next cell
  3. Repeat the steps until all Supply and Demand values are 0.

(Note that the null row is added to make supply and demand values equal.)

Steps for Least Cost Method:

  1. Choose the cell with the minimum unit cost, Cij, and allocate as much as possible, i.e., minimum (A1, B1)
    1. Take this minimum value and subtract it from supply Ai and demand Bi
    2. Cross that row if the supply Ai is 0, and cross that column if the demand Bi is 0.
    3. If the min unit cost cell is not unique, choose the cell with the highest possible allocation.
  3. Repeat the steps until all Supply and Demand values are 0.

(Note that the null row is added to make supply and demand values equal.)

remember, no allocation can be negative (-) but might be 0.

Vogel’s Approximation Method steps:

  1. Start with calculating the difference of small cost and next higher to the smallest cost for each row and column. These differences are called the row and column penalties respectively
  2. Take the difference of two smallest numbers in each raw and write it in the row penalty
  3. Take the difference of the two smallest numbers in each column and write it in the column penalty
  4. Select the highest penalty and observe the corresponding row and column then allocate min (Ai, Bj) in the cell having the lowest cost in the select row and column.
  5. Ignored satisfied row or column for further consideration. Calculate fresh penalties for remaining sub metrics (m) in the same manner
  6. Repeat the steps until all Supply and Demand values are 0.

(Note that the null row is added to make supply and demand values equal)
